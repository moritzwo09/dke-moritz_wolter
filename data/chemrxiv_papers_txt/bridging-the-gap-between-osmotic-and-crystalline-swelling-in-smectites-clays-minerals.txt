Abstract

16

The swelling behavior of smectite clay minerals has been extensively investigated due to their

17

critical role in construction stability. Two distinct swelling regimes have long been recognized:

18

crystalline swelling, involving the incorporation of a few water sheets between clay layers, and

19

osmotic swelling, characterized by the complete delamination of individual clay layers. Crystalline

20

swelling mechanisms have been well established through hydration techniques applied to dry clay

21

in a humid environment, while osmotic swelling has been observed through the gradual

22

concentration of a diluted clay suspension into a more compact, glassy state. Although a linear

23

transition between these two regimes has been proposed, it has yet to be experimentally

24

demonstrated. In this study, we investigate the complete dehydration process of a levitated smectite

25

suspension droplet using in-situ Small Angle X-ray Scattering (SAXS). The results reveal a gradual

26

shift from osmotic swelling to crystalline swelling, marked by a transition from a pure nematic

27

glass to a coexistence zone where the nematic phase contracts and a saturated crystalline phase

28

emerges, ultimately progressing to an unsaturated crystalline state. This transition occurs through

29

a continuous process in which the interlayer space decreases from four water layers to zero water

30

layers, forming stratified interlayers along the way.

Introduction

35

Clay minerals are lamellar aluminosilicates, naturally present in most soils and used since early

36

prehistoric times for their unique physical and chemical properties [1]. Among these properties,

37

some clay minerals have the ability to accommodate water within their sheets, leading to significant

38

swelling properties. These swelling clay minerals, such as the smectite group, are constituted by

39

2:1 (TOT) layer whose structure consists of a parallel stacking of one octahedral sheet (O)

40

sandwiched between two tetrahedral sheets (T). The presence of isomorphic substitutions by

41

cations of lower valency induces a permanent negative structural charge, either in the tetrahedral

42

or in the octahedral sheet. This charge is compensated by cations present in the interlayer space

43

with a charge per half unit cell ranging from 0.2 to 0.6 [2]. The swelling capacity of smectites then

44

strongly depends on the solvation of these counterions, which is a direct result of their nature and

45

their valency, as well as the ionic strength of the aqueous medium [3]. Swelling properties have

46

been long recognized, arousing the interest not only of geologists but also of chemists, physicists

47

and biologists from academia and industry due to their numerous industrial and environmental

48

applications. Notably, swelling properties of smectites are used to predict the impact of soil

49

modification due to moisture on constructions, for their use as barriers for the disposal of industrial

50

or nuclear wastes or as additives in drilling mud in the petroleum industry [4,5].

51

The swelling of smectites is divided into two main regimes most often qualified as crystalline and

52

osmotic swellings [6‚Äì8]. The layer-to-layer distance for smectites at the dry state (0W layer) is

53

around 10 √Ö. Since the pioneer works of Bradley et al. (1937), the crystalline swelling corresponds

54

to a 1D expansion of the clay layer-to-layer distance by incorporation of water sheets into the

55

interlayer space [9]. It is now well-established that the interlayer space can incorporate several

56

water sheets, increasing the layer-to-layer distance to 12.3-12.7 √Ö for one (1W), 15.2-15.8 √Ö for

57

two (2W) and 18.0-19 √Ö for three sheets of water (3W) depending on the nature of the interlayer

58

cation (size, valency) [10‚Äì14]. The possibility of a 4W hydration state with layer-to-layer distance

59

of 21.4-22 √Ö has been reported since the forties but remains rarely observed, only in systems of

60

montmorillonite samples (low-charged smectite with octahedral layer charge) [7,15,16]. The

61

second regime is the so-called osmotic swelling, which occurs at higher water content. This state

62

corresponds to a complete delamination of the material into individual platelets, typically by
3

https://doi.org/10.26434/chemrxiv-2025-fgmbq ORCID: https://orcid.org/0000-0002-6776-7201 Content not peer-reviewed by ChemRxiv. License: CC BY-NC-ND 4.0

63

dispersing powder samples into ultrapure water [17,18]. When the water content is increased, there

64

is a difference in ion concentration between the interlayer space and the bulk water, resulting in the

65

formation of an osmotic pressure gradient and a reduction of the platelets cohesion [19]. In addition,

66

the cations present in the interlayer space are hydrated and therefore mobile, meaning that water

67

molecules can penetrate the interlayer space to balance the difference in osmotic pressure. The

68

osmotic swelling is possible only for low bonding energy cations, i.e., in the case of smectites

69

homoionized with monovalent cations such as Li+ or Na+ [7,20]. The layer-to-layer distance

70

increases until the platelets are fully dispersed, forming colloidal sols whose stability is governed

71

by the Derjaguin-Landau-Verwey-Overbeek (DLVO) theory, with the range of electrostatic

72

interactions defined by the Debye length. The colloidal stability of smectites has also been

73

extensively studied theoretically, with numerous models proposed to explain particle organization

74

in the dilute regime (3D osmotic swelling). In this regime, the system initially exhibits an isotropic

75

phase, which may transition into a nematic liquid crystal phase before evolving into an arrested

76

state with nematic-like ordering (1D osmotic swelling), typically occurring at volume fractions of

77

a few percent.

78

To probe the structure evolution, the most appropriate approach is to perform time resolved small-

79

angle X-ray (SAXS) or neutron scattering experiments [21‚Äì23] during hydration or drying.

80

Smectites have been studied through two distinct approaches, either by adding water to the system

81

or by removing it. As early as 1954, Norrish [7] suggested a linear evolution between these two

82

regimes. However, this fundamental question has never been explored experimentally so far owing

83

to the difficulty in investigating the transition between fully dispersed and fully dry nanosheet states

84

through a single experiment. We propose here a novel strategy to study the swelling of clay

85

minerals, revealing the transition from fully dispersed to fully dried smectite platelets by

86

continuous evaporation. Among recent developments, acoustic levitation [24,25] emerges as an

87

effective approach for conducting in-situ studies on self-standing droplets, enabling real-time

88

kinetic analysis. The immobilization of droplets at the nodes of an acoustic standing wave, can be

89

experimentally achieved between an ultrasonic transducer and a reflector [26,27]. The combination

90

of SAXS with acoustic levitation can address a variety of scientific challenges, including the

91

investigation of the self-assembly of colloids or proteins [28‚Äì30], out-of-equilibrium phenomena

92

and revisiting phase diagrams [31].

In this study, the dehydration process of a levitated droplet of smectite suspensions is investigated

94

using time-resolved Small Angle X-ray Scattering coupled with an acoustic levitator (LevSAXS).

95

In this configuration, we have been able to fully capture the evolution of layer-to-layer distances

96

from the dilute regime (isotropic/nematic transition and liquid/glass transition) to the concentrated

97

crystalline regime and ultimately the dry state. Notably, this investigation reveals the gradual

98

transition from osmotic to crystalline swelling, defining the various hydration states of smectites,

99

details the evolution of layer-to-layer distances, the relative proportions of platelets involved in

100

each process, and the transitions between these states.

Results

103

Osmotic swelling regime

104

We selected a standard beidellite from Idaho (SBId-1, i.e., low-charged smectite with tetrahedral

105

layer charge), which is known to display a rich colloidal behaviour in dilute conditions with a

106

transition from isotropic to nematic liquid-crystalline phase before forming an arrested phase for œÜ

107

~ 1% [32]. Before use, the clay sample is purified of accessory minerals and Na-saturated according

108

to previously established procedure [33,34]. Then, a size fractionation is applied by successive

109

centrifugation at various speed and redispersion in ultrapure water, resulting in a dispersion of

110

discoidal particles with a mean diameter of 210 nm (polydispersity of 38%) and an average

111

thickness of 0.6 nm [32]. The volume fraction (œÜ) of nanosheets is adjusted by dilution of a

112

concentrated stock solution obtained by osmotic stress [35].

113

Upon evaporation of a diluted smectite suspension, the system first evolves through the 3D osmotic

114

swelling regime, followed by an 1D crystalline compression until the dry state. During the osmotic

115

swelling regime, liquid-crystalline phase may appear. Indeed, suspensions of plate like particles

116

can exhibit liquid-crystalline phase transition depending on the aspect ratio as volume fraction

117

increases due to a competition between orientational entropy and packing entropy governed by

118

excluded volume interactions [36‚Äì38]. At thermodynamic equilibrium, beidellite suspension

119

exhibit an isotropic/nematic (I/N) transition in the liquid state [32,39]. Above a critical volume

120

fraction, the platelets show rotational and translational arrested motion into a glass-like state

121

displaying a nematic-like ordering. The observation of such phases is typically observed after long

Crystalline swelling

199

As the system dries, the layer-to-layer distance decreases, shifting the nematic peak towards higher

200

Q-values. At a certain stage, clearly defined diffraction peaks appear on the SAXS curves in

201

addition to the characteristic nematic glass pattern (Figure 4A and Figures S3, Videos S4-S5).

202

This corresponds to the crystalline compression, i.e., face-to-face stacks of clay layers with water

203

sheets in between. Before reaching the complete dry state (i.e., 0W layer, with a layer-to-layer

204

distance of ~10 √Ö), the system undergoes a dehydration process marked by complex transitions,

205

with the presence of different discrete hydration states.

212

shown that the crystalline dehydration is a continuous process due to layer charge heterogeneities,

213

214

3. Conclusion

276

In summary, the dehydration mechanism of smectite has been investigated by levSAXS, allowing

277

to probe experimentally and theoretically the linear evolution between the osmotic and the

278

crystalline swelling regime. Upon drying the levitated droplet, we first observe an isotropic to

279

nematic phase transition. This transition is heterogeneous inside the droplet as we observe a

280

splitting of the characteristic nematic peak. The assumption is the occurrence of a faster drying at

281

the edges of the droplet rather than in the ‚Äúbulk‚Äù center. At a certain stage of the drying process, a

282

crystalline state emerges, with a continuous evolution leading to the coexistence of interstratified

283

layers. The process reflects a gradual progression from osmotic swelling to crystalline swelling,

284

characterized by a transition from a pure nematic glass to a coexistence zone where the nematic

285

phase contracts and a saturated crystalline phase emerges, ultimately leading to an unsaturated

286

crystalline state. This transition occurs through a continuous process, where the layer-to-layer space

287

evolves from containing four layers of water (4W) down to 0W, with stratified interlayers.
