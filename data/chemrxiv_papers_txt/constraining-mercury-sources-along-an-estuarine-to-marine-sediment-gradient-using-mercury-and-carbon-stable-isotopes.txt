Abstract

19

The coastal ocean receives more mercury from riverine input (land-derived Hg) than from direct

20

atmospheric deposition. Land-derived Hg is primarily particle-bound and buried in coastal sediment,

21

where it may undergo methylation. However, its contribution to coastal sediment Hg loads remains

22

poorly constrained.

23

Here, we investigate Hg sources along an estuarine-to-marine gradient in the Bay of Biscay (Atlantic

24

Ocean) combining Hg and carbon (C) stable isotopic composition in sediments. We find a significant

25

(p<0.05) difference in Î´202Hg and Î´13C between estuarine (Î´202Hg -0.83 Â± 0.15â€°, Î´13C -27.3 Â± 0.50â€°)

26

and marine (Î´202Hg -0.54 Â± 0.16â€°, Î´13C -25.2 Â± 0.80â€°) sediments, and a significant correlation

27

(p<0.05) between Î´13C and Î´202Hg. While in-situ processes had a negligible impact on Hg isotopic

28

composition, the observed trends could be explained by the mixing of land-derived Hg (e.g.

29

vegetational uptake, anthropogenic) and direct atmospheric deposition to the marine environment.

30

Applying a binary-mixing model with regional endmembers to Î´13C and Î´202 signatures reveals a near

31

1:1 relationship between land-derived Hg and terrestrial C along our estuarine-to-marine gradient. This

32

relationship, if confirmed for other regions, suggests that joint observations of C stable isotopes and

33

Hg concentrations could directly constrain the transfer of land-derived Hg into coastal sediments.

1. Introduction

46

Estuarine and coastal zones are highly dynamic transition areas between land and sea, receiving

47

continuous riverine input of nutrients, suspended particulate matter, and trace metals including

48

mercury (Hg). The majority of riverine Hg is associated with particulate organic carbon [1,2] and buried

49

in sediments trough particle settling [3,4]. In sediments, Hg may undergo microbial methylation to

50

form methylated Hg (MeHg) [5,6], a potent neurotoxin, which magnifies along the trophic chain

51

resulting in high exposure levels for fish consuming populations [7â€“9].

52

For the global coastal ocean, riverine inputsof Hg exceed atmospheric deposition [2]. While reduction

53

of riverine inorganic Hg (Hg(II)) and its evasion to the atmosphere may partially offset the input of

54

terrestrial Hg to the coastal ocean [10], it has been estimated to be of minor importance [2,11,12],

55

likely due to the importance of transient flood events in particle export [2,13].

56

However, these estimates are highly uncertain, as Earth system models lack adequate representation

57

of estuarine and coastal processes [2], and observational data covering the estuarine-to-marine

58

continuum remain scarce [14â€“17]. In light of the projected increase in river flood events with climate

59

change, which will likely elevate the Hg burden to coastal zones, and the potential for continental shelf

60

regions to hold 3 to 4 times more Hg than previously estimated, it is critical to better constrain the fate

61

of Hg over the estuarine-to-marine continuum [2].

62

Hg strongly binds to reduced sulfur sites in organic matter in aquatic environments [18], influencing its

63

transport, transformations and bioavailability [7,19,20]. In riverine and estuarine systems, most Hg is

64

bound to particles [2,21â€“24], and this is also observed in the Adour estuary [10,25]. The strong

65

association of Hg with organic C has proven valuable in models constraining riverine Hg export to the

66

ocean [2].

67

Distinct C stable isotopic signatures (Î´Â¹Â³C) serves as a tracer for C origin and cycling, with terrestrial

68

particulate organic matter typically exhibiting lighter values (i.e. -27â€° to -30â€°) and phytoplankton-

69

derived C (marine) showing heavier values (i.e.-20â€° to -25â€°) [26].

70

Similarly, stable isotopic composition of Hg can be used constrain sources and/or characterize

71

biogeochemical transformation processes in different matrices, such as marine sediments where Hg is

72

ultimately buried [14,16,17,27â€“30]. Hg has seven stable isotopes, which undergo mass-dependent

73

(MDF) and mass-independent fractionation (MIF) during partial biogeochemical processes, resulting in

74

distinct signatures (Î´202Hg, Î”199Hg, Î”200Hg, Î”201Hg, Î”204Hg) that reflect different sources and/or

75

processes [31,32]. While all studied biogeochemical processes [31,32] induce mass dependent

76

fractionation (MDF, Î´202Hg), large mass-independent fractionation of odd Hg isotopes (odd-MIF,

77

Î”199Hg, Î”201Hg) is predominantly of photochemical origin [33] due to the magnetic isotope effect (MIE)

78

[34]. Another mechanism inducing odd-MIF, though of smaller magnitude, is the nuclear volume effect

79

(NVE) [35]. In contrast, even-MIF (Î”200Hg, Î”204Hg) is considered a conservative tracer because it is

80

thought to be exclusively generated by photochemical reactions in the upper troposphere and/or

81

stratosphere [36,37]. Atmospheric Hg(0) and Hg(II) in precipitation exhibit distinct Î”200Hg signatures,

82

allowing the differentiation of atmospheric deposition pathways [38].

83

Here we investigate a gradient of surface sediments collected from the upstream Adour river estuary

84

seaward including the initial 23.5 km of the Capbreton canyon (Bay of Biscay, Atlantic). High total Hg

85

concentrations have been reported (> 500 ng g-1) for the Capbreton canyon [5,39] which were found

86

to be correlated with the carbon-rich fine fraction [5,39] and carbon stable isotopic composition (Î´13C)

87

[5]. This continuum offers a unique opportunity to investigate Hg export and cycling along the

88

transition zone from estuarine-to-marine environments. By combining stable isotopic composition of

89

Hg and C, we aim to constrain geochemical processes and identify primary Hg sources along this unique

90

land-ocean transition zone.

2. Material and Methods

92

2.1. Sampling Site

93

The Adour estuary and the adjacent Capbreton canyon are located in the south eastern Bay of Biscay

94

(northeastern Atlantic Ocean, France). The Adour estuary is a mesotidal estuary within an urbanized

95

area, while agriculture and livestock activities dominate the upstream and watershed resulting in

96

different levels of anthropogenic impact [40]. The mean annual discharge is around 300 m3 s-1 but can

97

reach more than 2000 m3 s-1 during brief flood events [10,25,41], which makes the Adour the third

98

largest freshwater inflow into the Bay of Biscay [42]. The residence time of particles in the estuary is

99

short (hours to days) compared to estuaries with a stronger marine influence [13,43â€“45]. It has been

100

estimated that 75 % of the annual suspended matter flux occurs over 30 â€“ 40 days during flooding

101

events [13]. Although the Capbreton canyon has naturally been disconnected from the Adour river

102

system in 1310 AD, the Adour remains the main sediment provider for the canyon, with active infill

103

and particle remobilization processes still ongoing [46â€“48].

104

The Adour estuary and adjacent marine area has been extensively studied for Hg contamination and

105

potential Hg transformations [5,10,25,40,41,44,49]. It has been shown that Hg water concentrations

106

within the estuary decreased with anthropogenic Hg emissions over the past 20 years [25]. However,

107

high levels of Hg (up to 975 ng g-1) have been shown to accumulate on the slopes and terraces of the

108

canyon, along with fine, organic-rich particles [5].

2.3. Total Hg Concentration Measurements and Ancillary Parameters

126

All samples were homogenized and freeze-dried before further handling and ground with an agate

127

mortar and pestle before digestion and total Hg analysis (THg). THg concentrations and ancillary

128

parameters for Adour and Capbreton samples and the corresponding analytical procedures have been

129

published elsewhere [5,41]. In brief, THg concentrations were determined on 50 â€“ 100 mg of ground

130

samples by atomic absorption spectrophotometry (AAS) on an Advanced Mercury Analyzer (AMA-254,

131

132

inorganic Hg determined by species-specific isotope dilution GC ICP-MS on sediment digests. Detection

133

limit for THg in sediments based on three times the standard deviation (SD) of the digestion blank

134

(Capbreton) was better than 0.07 ng g-1 dry weight or three times the SD of blank measurements with

135

3 ng g-1 dry weight (Adour). Accuracy and precision were determined by analyzing international

136

certified materials of riverine and marine sediments (IAEA405/433, and BCR320R/580). For both

137

analytical methods, mean recoveries/concentrations were within the 95 % confidence interval of

138

certified values.

139

Total carbon (TC), particulate organic carbon (POC), carbon and nitrogen stable isotopes (Î´13C, Î´15N),

140

the C:N ratio and grain size distribution were determined on all samples as described previously [5,41].

2.5. Hg Burial rates

174

The Capbreton canyon is a large incision in the continental shelf characterized by its high sediment

175

export from the land to the deep ocean, where elevated accumulation rates (more than 1 cm yr-1) are

176

observed on terraces [39,48]. To estimate Hg burial rates on the Capbreton Canyon terraces, we used

177

dry bulk density and surface THg concentrations for two sediment cores (G09, G23), applying

178

conservative sedimentation rates of 0.19 and 0.45 cm yr-1 from literature for the Capbreton Canyon

179

(see SI section 1.3 for details).

274

276

278

279

285

290

3.4. Discrimination of Potential Hg Sources to Estuarine-to-Marine Sediments

357

In our system, Hg mainly originates from vegetational uptake (atmospheric Hg(0)), urban influence,

358

and precipitation (mostly Hg(II)). Both are stored in soils and enter the river system through runoff

359

before being exported to the coastal ocean [25,53]. In addition, Hg may be deposited to the ocean via

360

direct atmospheric deposition of Hg(0) (dry) and Hg(II) (wet and dry) [27]. Terrestrial Hg, i.e. Hg(0)

361

sequestered by foliage and subsequently oxidized, is imprinted with a significant (>2 â€°) shift towards

362

lighter Î´202Hg values [64], while post-depositional processes and Hg re-emission may induce a

363

significant shift in both Î´202Hg and Î”199Hg in soils or atmospheric accumulators [64].

364

The potential sources of Hg in sediments for our study area are compiled in Figure 4 along with local

365

isotopic signatures for lichens, mosses [65], soils [65], total gaseous mercury (here assumed equal to

366

Hg(0)), precipitation [38,66], and the global open ocean [27]. Dry Hg(0) deposition typically dominates

367

Hg signatures in soils and atmospheric accumulators, such as lichens and mosses. This was also

368

established for local (catchment area of the Adour river) lichens and mosses in the western Pyrenees

369

with an Hg(0) contribution of ~80 % on average (Figure SI 9) (Barre et al. 2018, 2020). A considerable

370

deviation from the linear regression of Î”Â¹â¹â¹Hg and Î”Â²â°â°Hg signatures for Hg(0) and precipitation

371

suggests that most local soil, lichen, and some moss samples were affected by post-depositional

372

processes (Figure 4B). This is exemplified by a mean negative Î”199Hgexc for soils and lichens (Figure SI

373

9), while Î”199Hgexc for mosses was more variable (Figure SI 8) [65].

374

For Adour and Capbreton sediment samples, however, we do not observe such deviation of Î”199Hg

375

from the theoretical mixing between Hg(0) and precipitation-derived Hg (Figure 4D). This is in line with

376

the previously established absence of Î”199Hgexc. The absence of Î”199Hgexc in Adour sediments suggests

377

that Hg in estuarine sediments has not undergone such post-depositional processes. However, it

378

cannot be excluded that another process or source may obscure the Î”199Hgexc signature, or that most

379

lichens, and to some extent soils samples do not represent our estuarine Hg endmember found in

380

Adour sediments (Î”199Hg, possibly Î´202Hg).

3.5. Implications for Hg Source Apportionment in Coastal Marine Systems

420

Our data suggest that biogeochemical processes had a minor influence on Hg isotopic composition and

421

that the observed differences in Î´202Hg along the estuarine to coastal-marine continuum are best

422

explained by a mixing of different Hg sources. To decipher the main sources of Hg in the study area,

423

we applied a two-endmember mixing model. The two endmembers were defined as land-derived Hg

424

i.e. derived from vegetational uptake and anthropogenic impact (lichens industrial sector), and direct

425

atmospheric deposition of Hg (open ocean).

426

We find a significant difference between sampling groups (Figure SI 13), with a higher land-derived Hg

427

contribution in Adour (70 Â± 19 %) compared to Capbreton samples (33 Â± 19 %). While the estimated

428

fraction of terrestrial Hg naturally depends on the defined endmembers, our results illustrate the

429

progressive dilution of land-derived Hg along the estuarine-marine sediment gradient.

430

It is notable that, if approximately 70% of Hg in sediment samples from the Adour estuary is land-

431

derived, around 30 % is not. This may be explained by a direct anthropogenic (urban) influence in these

432

sediments, the transport of marine Hg from the shelf into the Adour estuary (for instance through tidal

433

pumping), or a combination of both. However, due to the absence of Hg isotopic composition

434

difference in the â€œdirect anthropogenicâ€ and â€œmarineâ€ pools for our study area (both + MDF, as

435

obtained from literature), we are not able to resolve this question here.
