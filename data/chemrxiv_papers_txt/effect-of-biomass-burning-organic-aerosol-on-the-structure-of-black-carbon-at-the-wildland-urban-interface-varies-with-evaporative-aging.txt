Environmental significance

10

Wildland fires are a significant source of primary organic aerosol, called biomass burning

11

organic aerosol (BBOA). When this material crosses the wildland-urban interface (WUI),

12

it can interact with urban black carbon (BC), uncoated aggregates with fractal, highly

13

branched structures, emitted largely from diesel engines. Specifically, it can partition onto

14

BC, resulting in restructuring due to surface tension and capillary forces. Here, we show

15

in saturator-condenser experiments that fresh BBOA from sources close to the WUI is

16

more effective by volume at restructuring BC than evaporatively aged BBOA from sources

17

far from the WUI. The resulting morphologies dictate how the particles will interact with

18

radiation and water vapor, interactions that govern their direct and indirect effects on

19

climate, respectively.

Abstract

22

Biomass burning organic aerosol (BBOA) and black carbon (BC) play significant roles in

23

climate, via their interactions with light and clouds, as well as public health. BC in the

24

urban atmosphere, largely emitted by diesel vehicles, is initially highly branched and

25

fractal, but it can be restructured upon coating. Urban BC aggregates can be exposed to

26

the components of BBOA when wildfire plumes cross the wildland-urban interface (WUI),

27

which may partition to coat the BC. Here, we investigate the effects of laboratory-

28

generated BBOA from pine on BC from an inverted diffusion burner. Initially 200 nm BC

29

aggregates were selected using a differential mobility analyzer (DMA) and exposed to

30

different volatility fractions of BBOA. These fractions were prepared by evaporative aging

31

in the laboratory, which mimics evaporative aging with transport in the atmosphere, to

32

give fresh, moderately aged, and most aged BBOA. The resulting mobility diameters with

33

and without removal of the coating were determined using a second DMA. We find that

34

an exceedingly small coating volume of fresh (i.e., higher volatility) BBOA causes complete

35

restructuring while a significantly higher coating volume of most aged (i.e., lower

36

volatility) BBOA is necessary for this same extent of restructuring. Our results highlight

37

the importance of evaporative age of BBOA as wildfire plumes are transported across the

38

WUI into urban environments.

1. Introduction

41

Black carbon (BC) aggregates affect air quality as well as the radiative balance of the

42

atmosphere, through interactions with light and clouds.1 BC aggregates are composed of

43

spherical primary particles of elemental carbon arranged in highly branched, fractal

44

morphologies,2,3 which dictate how the particles interact with light. 4,5 BC aggregates may

45

also accumulate coatings that alter these interactions through lensing effects. 6‚Äì8 These

46

coatings may induce restructuring of the BC aggregates, resulting in more compact,

47

closely-packed morphologies.9 Coatings that have been observed to induce BC

48

restructuring in laboratory experiments include water,10,11 sulphuric acid,12,13 and

49

secondary organic aerosol (SOA) from a wide range of gaseous precursors: e.g., biogenic

50

isoprene and Œ±-pinene;14‚Äì17 anthropogenic benzene,18 toluene,18,19 ethylbenzene,18 and

51

m- and p-xylene,18,20,21 and co-emitted combustion byproducts.22,23 Water is important

52

also for its ability to plasticize solid organic coatings or deliquesce solid inorganic coatings,

53

such that the liquified coatings can go on to induce restructuring.24,25 For some coatings,

54

like water, the mechanism of restructuring is evaporation of the coating; 10,26 for others,

55

the mechanism of restructuring is a combination of condensation and evaporation. 27‚Äì29

56

Several models of restructuring have been developed to represent the forces (e.g.,

57

capillary and surface tension) acting on the primary particles in coated BC aggregates. 20,30‚Äì

58

32 For initially monodisperse BC, the combination of restructuring and coating has been

59

observed to result in significant absorption enhancement.13,19,33 In the atmosphere,

60

coated BC aggregates occur across a wide range of morphologies, differing the amount of

61

BC (i.e., few or many primary particles), the amount of coating (i.e., thinly or thickly

coated), the compactness of the BC (i.e., branched or closely packed), and the distribution

63

of the coating on the BC (i.e., centric or acentric).34‚Äì36 This wide range of morphologies

64

further complicates the physical and optical properties of BC.37,38

65

BC is an important component of the urban atmosphere, where it is largely

66

emitted from diesel vehicles.39,40 The initial morphology of BC from diesel engines has

67

been investigated and shown to be highly branched.41‚Äì43 Branched aggregates produced

68

from diesel engines in the laboratory have been shown to be compacted upon

69

coating.44,45 Urban BC has also been shown to be coated and compacted in the field. 46‚Äì48

70

Nascent, monodisperse BC introduced into an outdoor semi-permeable chamber, where

71

it was exposed to gaseous species in two urban atmospheres, also was coated and

72

compacted.33 The occurrence of BC in the urban atmosphere that is initially largely bare

73

contrasts with BC in wildfire plumes that is initially internally mixed with organic aerosol,

74

called biomass burning organic aerosol (BBOA),49 which influences the physical and

75

optical properties of co-emitted BC as described above.50‚Äì53 Wildfires have been shown

76

to influence the organic composition of suspended particles and deposited films in the

77

urban environment;54,55 for example, wildfires increase the concentration of polycyclic

78

aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) in the urban atmosphere.55 Several PAHs have been shown

79

to restructure BC aggregates in the laboratory as sub-cooled liquids. 56 Consequently,

80

when wildfire plumes cross the wildland-urban interface (WUI), 57‚Äì59 urban BC is exposed

81

to the components of BBOA, which may alter the morphology of the BC aggregates.

82

Furthermore, BBOA is a complex mixture of organic species with wide ranges of physical

83

and chemical properties,60‚Äì62 which may influence its interactions with urban BC.

4. Atmospheric implications

347

Our results have broad implications on the interactions between BBOA from wildland fires

348

and BC from urban sources at the WUI. Each volatility fraction of BBOA induced

349

restructuring of BC aggregates upon partitioning, with the coating intact, demonstrating

350

that compaction occurs without coating evaporation. This observation stands in contrast

351

with restructuring induced by water, which requires evaporation, 10 and it is an important

352

feature of the interactions between BBOA and BC, since many BBOA components, with

353

lower volatilities, would not be expected to partition from the coated aggregates back

354

into the gas phase. The restructuring induced by the most aged BBOA also demonstrates

355

that even the most viscous volatility fraction acts as a liquid in the internally mixed

356

particles.24,56 Furthermore, the differences observed between the fresh, moderately

357

aged, and most aged BBOA demonstrate that volatility largely governs the impact of BBOA

358

coatings on BC aggregate morphology. Fresh BBOA, emitted from wildfires near the WUI,

359

is most effective at restructuring BC aggregates; i.e., coating volumes as little as 10% of

360

the initial BC volume are sufficient to induce complete restructuring of BC aggregates to

361

their final, most closely-packed morphology. As the evaporative age of BBOA increases,

362

for emissions of wildfires at increasing distances from the WUI, the BBOA becomes less
