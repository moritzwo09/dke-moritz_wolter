Abstract

9

Thirty two commercially available standards were used to determine chromatographic retention indices

10

(RIs) for three different stationary phases. The selected compounds were nitrogen-containing heterocycles

11

and amides, which are unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine (UDMH) transformation products or its assumed

12

transformation products. UDMH is a highly toxic compound widely used in the space industry, that forms

13

a number of different compounds in the environment. Well-known transformation products may exceed

14

UDMH itself in their toxicity, but most of the products are poorly investigated, while posing a huge

15

environmental threat. Experimental RIs for the stationary phases, RIs from the NIST database, and

16

predicted RIs are presented in this paper. It is shown that there are virtually no RIs for UDMH

17

transformation products in the NIST database. In addition, even among those compounds for which RIs

18

were known, inconsistencies were identified. Adding RIs to the database and eliminating erroneous data

19

would allow for more reliable identification when standards are not available. The discrepancies identified

20

between experimental RI values and predicted values will allow for adjustments to the machine learning

21

models that are used for prediction. Previously proposed compounds as possible transformation products

22

without the use of standards and NMR method were confirmed.

1. Introduction

27

Due to the rising amount of new chemicals released to the environment non-targeted analysis using

28

mass spectrometry (MS) coupled to liquid/gas chromatography (LC/GC) have risen as a new approach in

29

analytical chemistry to detect, identify and eventually quantify chemicals in environmental samples [1,2].

30

Especially non-targeted analysis is prevalent when working with unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine

31

(UDMH), which is a toxic component of rocket fuel. The particular complexity of UDMH as a research

32

object lies in its high reactivity, which leads to the formation of a large number of transformation products

33

[3,4]. Establishing the structure of new UDMH transformation products is a challenging task as standard

34

samples are in most cases unavailable. Also, various environmental factors, such as humidity, pH, matrix,
1

35

etc., influence the UDMH transformation, which leads to the fact that at the moment there is no precise

36

understanding of what substances will be formed under certain conditions [5,6]. The expansive distribution

37

of false transformation products should be mentioned, as often the identification of new compounds

38

belonging to transformation products is superficially performed only by the mass spectra obtained without

39

the use of additional methods [5,7]. In our work, we also observed that some compounds might not be

40

available in databases and identification was done indirectly based on available published data [8].

41

Majority of studies identify UDMH transformation products by a library search of the MS-spectra

42

database. At the same time, a database search in around 20% of times shows an incorrect result even if the

43

mass spectrum is available in the database. Nevertheless, the structure elucidation of isomeric compounds

44

based only on their fragmentation pattern, may sometimes not be feasible, since they produce common

45

fragments and the reference standards are not always available [9]. At the moment many substances known

46

as possible UDMH transformation products are not available in any databases [7,10]. Only several works

47

use standards [11] and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) [10,12]. At the same time, even the use of high-

48

resolution MS [3,13] does not allow to assume a possible structure, since tens of thousands of possible

49

structures with different properties can correspond to the brutto-formula. GC retention index (RI) can be

50

used as an additional factor to confirm or reject candidates during GC-MS identification [14]. However,

51

reference RIs are available for less than half of the compounds in the NIST database, and reference RIs are

52

not available for most UDMH transformation products [15]. This problem can be partially solved by

53

predicting RI using machine learning [16]. During the last decade prediction of RIs with machine learning

54

has been applied more and more widely to the problems of non-targeted analysis in environmental

55

chemistry [17‚Äì22]

56

The work of our team demonstrated the successful use of a combination of high-performance liquid

57

chromatography-high resolution mass spectrometry (HPLC-HRMS) and GC-MS with artificial intelligence

58

techniques to cross-check the proposed structures of new UDMH transformation products [7,15]. However,

59

here we would like to highlight a very important nuance that machine learning is conducted on known

60

databases, including NIST, and the accuracy of prediction directly depends on the correctness of the

61

available values. Khrisanfov et al. developed a novel approach to find potentially erroneous entries in a

62

large-scale database with mostly unique entries [23]. The authors [14,24] also raise concerns that some RI

63

values in the database may be erroneous. RI filtering in the NIST database is an important task because

64

about 80% of the data have only one RI value per stationary phase (SP) [23]. Identifying incorrect entries

65

will improve the prediction accuracy of machine learning models.

66

Therefore, the aim of this work was to compare experimental, reference (from the NIST database)

67

and predicted RIs obtained for different SP for possible UDMH transformation products. The experimental

68

RI and predicted RI were compared with those available in the literature. This approach is presented for the

69

first time on the example of UDMH transformation identification. For this purpose, standards of substances

70

were selected that are or are suspected to be UDMH transformation products or may be structurally similar

71

to its transformation products. Standard SP for the analysis of UDMH transformation products were chosen

for the experiment. Different types of SP ranging from non-polar (RTx-5MS) [25,26], mid-polar (DB-1701)

74

75

76

77

by us can be used to adjust the identification methods of unknown UDMH transformation products, which

78

will allow to make a more precise assessment of UDMH and its transformation product impact on the

79

environment.

4. Conclusions

251

For the first time, retention indices were calculated for some confirmed and assumed UDMH

252

transformation products for 3 different stationary phases - polar (HP-INNOWax), non-polar (RTx-5MS)

253

and medium polarity (DB-1701). In this work, for the first time, an approach was used where experimental

254

retention indices and predicted retention indices were compared with those available in the literature. For

255

a large number of confirmed UDMH transformation products there are no published values of retention

256

indices even for the non-polar phase. However, it should be noted that in the course of this study some

257

discrepancies were found with the records in the NIST database for the polar and nonpolar phases.

258

Erroneous values greatly affect the accuracy of the prediction, especially when the database is small, as

259

seen in the polar phase example. The predicted indices for the mid-polar stationary phase showed good

260

convergence with the experimental data. It is also worth noting that this phase showed the best separation

261

ability compared to the polar and non-polar phases, and these phases should be used to analyze UDMH

262

transformation products. The obtained data will allow to enrich the databases, which will subsequently

263

improve the accuracy of GC RI prediction. Our results can be used in non-target screening to adjust the

264

identification of compounds of emerging concerns that are not available as reference standards. This

265

approach would provide, by establishing the right structure, a more accurate assessment of the

266

environmental impact of UDMH and its transformation products.
