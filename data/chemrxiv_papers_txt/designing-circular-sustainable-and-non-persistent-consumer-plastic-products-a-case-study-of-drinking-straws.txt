ABSTRACT

17
18

Drinking straws present a simple form factor for evaluating material design strategies taken by

19

manufacturers to achieve circular, sustainable, and non-persistent products in response to global

20

restrictions and consumer sentiment. We investigated thirteen on-the-market drinking straws of

21

varying formulations, characterizing their physical, chemical, and marine biodegradation

22

properties. These data informed sustainability metrics used for evaluating the effectiveness and

23

tradeoffs of design strategies, ultimately arriving at four key conclusions. First, diverting

24

anthropogenic methane as a renewable feedstock to make polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA)

25

resulted in the only straw with a net-negative global warming potential. Second, adding biogenic

26

fillers to conventional polymers (e.g., polypropylene) to minimize plastic usage is unlikely to

27

produce substantial environmental benefits compared to using alternative polymers. Third, many

28

marketing claims about circularity, sustainability, and persistence were unsupported, likely

29

magnifying the mismanagement and environmental impacts of these products. Fourth, improper

30

disposal of compostable straws in landfills could increase the global warming potential of the item

31

by up to six times and offset numerous advantages afforded by biodegradable materials, thereby

32

warranting greater investment in waste management infrastructure. The analysis of design

33

strategies and their tradeoffs provided herein should be applied broadly when developing and

34

adopting future consumer products.

INTRODUCTION

42
43

Drinking straws are polarizing, frequently littered, and pervasive single-use products. Upwards of

44

50 billion drinking straws are used annually in the U.S.,1 and because these products are

45

improperly disposed of (mismanaged), they contribute to ~5% of coastal plastic debris globally.2

46

To curb their deleterious impacts as pollution, regulations have targeted drinking straws globally

47

from the municipal to federal levels, banning products perceived as persistent in the environment.

48

In response to these restrictions and consumer sentiment, a market for more sustainable and less

49

persistent drinking straws emerged. In recent years, the market has ballooned to include dozens

50

of brands (Table S1) claiming to be less impactful to the environment. Such claims are predicated

51

on the switch to plastic formulations using different polymers, such as polyhydroxyalkanoates

52

(PHAs), and additives, such as organic and inorganic fillers.

53
54

Environmentally conscious consumers, businesses, and policymakers face challenges navigating

55

advertising for these new drinking straws.3 The details provided by brands and manufacturers

56

range from cryptic and vacuous marketing language to informative and readily accessible

57

messaging. This fact is belabored by the myriad certifications and labels on product packaging

58

that require a detailed understanding of their test methods to fully appreciate the nuances and

59

reasonable claims that can be drawn from them. Two of the most frequently used labels are from

60

certifiers TÃ¼v Austria and the Biodegradable Products Institute (BPI),4,5 which provide certificates

61

for home and industrial compostability. These third parties certify polymer resins and products

62

according to compostability standards (e.g., ASTM 64006), as well as their additional scrutiny.

63

Other labels reflect claims of feedstock renewability and global warming potential (GWP). The

64

emphasis on certification is a legal matter (e.g., under the U.S. Federal Trade Commission Act7).

https://doi.org/10.26434/chemrxiv-2025-vtczp ORCID: https://orcid.org/0000-0002-6104-8310 Content not peer-reviewed by ChemRxiv. License: CC BY 4.0

65

Certification is necessary for businesses to substantiate any environmental marketing claims,

66

particularly those related to degradability.

67
68

Addressing persistence has often been met by designing straws for degradation in engineered

69

environments, such as compost. This attribute is necessary for circular material schemes

70

following a managed life cycle. Yet, the safeguard solution to the pollution of frequently

71

mismanaged items is to imbue the items with inherent degradability in all environments:

72

engineered and natural.8 This feature has been advocated as one of six key interventions for

73

mitigating the potential impacts of plastic pollution by embracing the principles of green chemistry

74

and engineering in material and product design.9â€“11

75
76

While many drinking straws are certified as compostable and claim biodegradability, few present

77

evidence for their degradation in the marine environment, where these items are ubiquitous.12

78

Many compostable polymers feature incongruent degradation behavior with natural environments

79

(e.g., polylactic acid, PLA).13â€“15 Additionally, while certificates for marine biodegradability exist, the

80

methods for evaluating marine degradation do not represent natural conditions because of well-

81

recognized bottle effects.13 In short, these methods provide evidence that marine microbes can

82

biodegrade a material, but they are incapable of estimating lifetimes in natural settings. Having

83

intrinsic biodegradability in the marine environment is necessary for plastic products, such as

84

drinking straws, that frequently leak into the ocean.8

85
86

Plastic pollution is not the only environmental impact drinking straw manufacturers have aimed to

87

avoid and reduce by switching to alternative plastic formulations. Impacts across the entire life

88

cycle are being addressed to varying extents. The approaches manufacturers take to reduce the

89

life cycle environmental impacts of drinking straws represent a microcosm of the broader

90

landscape of approaches tried by the plastics industry to address the impacts of feedstock and

https://doi.org/10.26434/chemrxiv-2025-vtczp ORCID: https://orcid.org/0000-0002-6104-8310 Content not peer-reviewed by ChemRxiv. License: CC BY 4.0

91

end-of-life (EoL) disposal. Thus, drinking straws present a simple form factor (a hollow cylinder)

92

invariant across brands for quantifying the environmental impacts of different commercially-viable

93

material design strategies applied at scale for achieving sustainable plastic products.

94
95

Herein, drinking straws of varying formulations from different manufacturers were investigated for

96

their environmental impacts. These straws encompass products manufactured by startups to

97

multinational corporations being adopted by local stores and international brands alike. The

98

straws were evaluated for their physical, chemical, and realistic marine biodegradation properties

99

(i.e., lifetimes and microbial community composition), which informed sustainability metrics

100

related to renewability, material efficiency, resource utilization, GWP, and environmental

101

persistence. This analysis identified successful design strategies for sustainability and circularity,

102

as well as their tradeoffs that should be applied broadly in designing future plastic products.

Biodegradation under coastal marine conditions

312
313

To establish the potential for marine biodegradation, oxygen respiration of the microbes

314

associated with the straws was assayed. Respiration rates greater than the seawater-only

315

baseline provide evidence that the associated microbes can biodegrade the straws. These short-

316

term bottle incubations over three weeks showed that the PHA1-3, Resin 1, and paper straws

317

stimulated microbial respiration to varying degrees above the baseline (Table S6), supporting

318

marine biodegradability. The Resin 1 and paper straws had comparable respiration rates, about

319

double those of the PHA1-3 straws. The PP-based straws (PP, CaCO3 filled Resin, and Agave

320

Bagasse filled Resin straws) had respiration rates comparable to seawater, discounting their

321

marine biodegradability. The Resin 2 straw was unable to be assayed due to complications with

322

the poisoning agent (Section S2).

323
324

While short-term bottle incubations help establish whether microbes can degrade a polymer, open

325

326

a polymer over other available carbon sources in coastal settings (e.g., natural organic matter).

327

328

330

332

333

DISCUSSION

394
395

Drinking straws reflect the broader challenges to create products that i) work as intended, ii) meet

396

consumer preferences, iii) are inexpensive, and now iv) satisfy circularity, sustainability, and

397

degradability goals. Achieving these objectives for a drinking straw, an arguably simple product is

398

not so simple, as evidenced by the different straws investigated in this study. Each product

399

satisfies these four criteria to varying degrees and via different approaches. For the first three

400

criteria, each straw works as intended, meets consumer preferences, and is inexpensive to

401

differing extents (Section S4).

402
403

To satisfy circularity, sustainability, and degradability goals, each straw approached them through

404

different combinations of feedstock, plastic formulation, and EoL management. All but the Resin

405

2 straw was included in this analysis because of a lack of reliable data for this material.

406

Nonetheless, a brief general discussion on the sustainability of seaweed-based materials is

407

presented in Section S5. Additionally, the straws investigated in our previous study 14 (CDA, PLA,

408

PHA4, and PHA5, see Section S1) were included to reflect the broader design space of drinking

409

straws.

Feedstock renewability

412
413

To fit into a circular economy framework, a renewable feedstock is one in which the carbon has

414

been recirculated, including carbon obtained from biomass, industrial by-products, waste CO2 or

415

CH4, or recycled plastics.55 Most straws were made from 100% renewable materials (Figure 3A).

416

Only those with fossil-based polymers (i.e., PP and Resin 1) were partially or entirely made from

417

non-renewable materials.

418
419

The investigated PP-based straws appear to use comparable amounts of polymer to that of a

420

pure PP straw, negating the otherwise would be benefit of the renewably sourced filler. PP is

421

primarily produced using non-renewable petroleum sources, and ~5.5% is recycled. Biogenic

422

fillers (i.e., oyster-derived CaCO3 and agave-derived bagasse) can reduce the total amount of

423

polymer by replacing it with a renewable material. For instance, the CaCO3 filled Resin had an

424

average mass of ~746 mg, being ~18 wt% CaCO 3 meant that ~612 mg of the straw was PP,

425

effectively the same amount of material as the pure PP straw (~615 mg).

426
427

PHAs are produced by fermentation, using various carbon sources as feedstocks.56 The PHA

428

used in the PHA1 straw uses CH4 diverted from anthropogenic sources (e.g., wastewater, cattle

429

farming, landfills, and coal mining) as the carbon source, yielding a carbon offsetting material. In

430

contrast, the PHA2-5 straws use agricultural products, such as vegetable oils, to produce their

431

PHA materials. PHA2 was not 100% renewable because of its fraction of CaCO3 of unknown

432

origin (assumed non-biogenic).

433
434

Similar to PHAs, PLA is synthesized using lactic acid produced by the fermentation of agricultural

435

products.57 Therefore, PHAs and PLA made using these feedstocks can be certified as 100% bio-

https://doi.org/10.26434/chemrxiv-2025-vtczp ORCID: https://orcid.org/0000-0002-6104-8310 Content not peer-reviewed by ChemRxiv. License: CC BY 4.0

436

based. The Resin 1 straw was determined to be a blend of PLA and PBAT, and so was partially

437

bio-based, owing to its PLA content. PBAT is predominantly fossil-based, but efforts are being

438

made to circularize its feedstocks to include those from recycled materials and bio-based sources.

439
440

Historically, CDA has been a mixed feedstock wherein the cellulose was bio-derived, and the

441

carbon within the acetyl modification was derived from coal. Recognizing the non-renewability of

442

coal as a carbon source, more sustainable grades of CDA are being produced by deriving the

443

acetyl C from molecularly recycled mixed waste.58

Material efficiency

446
447

Material efficiency quantifies the amount of material used to make a functional product. 59

448

Optimizing the material efficiency of a plastic product has the added benefit of reducing many

449

other environmental impacts (e.g., GHG emissions and freshwater usage) because these impacts

450

are largely proportional to the amount of material used. 60â€“62 Material efficiency also affects

451

environmental impacts related to shipping because fuel efficiency decreases with increasing

452

cargo weight by a nontrivial amount.59,63 Because all the straws have the same function, their

453

mass is a measure of their material efficiency. The PHA1 straw had the greatest material

454

efficiency, followed by the PP straw (Figure 3B). The PHA1 straw used ~33% less material than

455

the PP straw. The other PHA and PP-based straws were less materially efficient, using more

456

material to produce a functional product. The paper straw was one of the least efficient, requiring

457

~60% more material than the PP straw.

Embodied GHG Emissions

460

https://doi.org/10.26434/chemrxiv-2025-vtczp ORCID: https://orcid.org/0000-0002-6104-8310 Content not peer-reviewed by ChemRxiv. License: CC BY 4.0

461

A first estimate for the GHG emissions of a product are the emissions associated with the

462

production of its materials. Because the specific embodied GHG emissions of plastics are within

463

an order of magnitude of each other, as were the masses of each straw, differences in GHG

464

emissions between products were expected to be marginal.26 This was not the case.

465
466

PHA1 was the only straw with net-negative embodied GHG emissions (Figure 3C), owing to

467

diverted CH4 emissions as its feedstock. Scaling this approach could impact the global C cycle

468

and climate change. For instance, if all the C used in plastic packaging globally (~142 Mt/yr 64)

469

were P3HB (~56 wt% C) and came from anthropogenic CH4, then ~71 Mt CH4 (~2.2 Gt CO2-eq)

470

would be needed. Adopting this strategy would offset ~20% of the ~9.7 Gt CO2-eq of annual global

471

anthropogenic CH4 emissions.65 Conversely, the other straws were all carbon-positive, ranging

472

from 0.80 kg CO2-eq/1000 straws for the paper to 4.16 kg CO2-eq/1000 straws for the Resin1,

473

PLA/PBAT-based straw (Figure 3C).

474
475

Relative to selecting materials with more renewable feedstocks, the effectiveness of biogenic

476

fillers appears negligible. The theoretical maximum offset in GHG emissions for oyster-derived

477

CaCO3 is ~0.44 kg CO2-eq/kg CaCO3. In practice, this value is likely reduced as GHG emissions

478

can be associated with the processing of the oyster shells to yield CaCO 3 of a quality suitable for

479

compounding. Based on its formulation, the theoretical maximum reduction in embodied GHG

480

emissions compared to the PP straw is only 4% for the CaCO 3 filled Resin straw. The value of the

481

theoretical specific embodied GHG emissions for agave-derived bagasse is greater than oyster-

482

derived CaCO3, offsetting ~1.51 kg CO2-eq/kg agave-derived bagasse. As with oyster-derived

483

CaCO3, this value is likely less because of GHG emissions associated with processing the

484

material. While the amount of agave-derived bagasse used in the Agave Bagasse filled Resin

485

straw was not disclosed, biocomposites of agave-derived bagasse and PP typically include 20-

486

30 wt% bagasse. Within this range, the estimated reduction in embodied GHG emissions for the

487

Agave Bagasse filled Resin straw compared to the PP straw was 8 to 28%.

Water usage

490
491

An impact of growing concern is a materialâ€™s embodied water usage because of the scarcity and

492

diminishing quality of freshwater resources globally.66,67 Generally, plastics derived from

493

agricultural products have greater embodied water usage than petroleum-derived plastics, owing

494

to the water used in agriculture and downstream processing. However, these impacts can be an

495

order of magnitude less than those resulting from paper production. Paper and pulp mills pollute

496

freshwater ecosystems and diminish their value, as well.68 Reported values for the embodied

497

water usage of PP, PHA, and paper are 32 L/kg, 240 L/kg, and 1700 L/kg, respectively. 69 Thus,

498

while the paper straw can have some of the lowest embodied GHG emissions (~2-3 times less

499

than the others, excluding PHA1), it can have substantial embodied water penalties (~10-80 times

500

more than the others) (Figure 3D). Water is a valuable natural resource; thus, reducing its usage

501

as much as possible will benefit society.70,71

EoL (mis)management and GWP

504
505

Managed EoL. The strategies advertised and promoted for the managed EoL of the straws varied

506

with the material. The PP-based straws were intended to follow a linear EoL path with disposal in

507

landfills. The PHA-based, paper, and Resin 2 straws were intended to follow a more circular EoL

508

path with disposal in industrial or home composting systems. The Resin 1 straw was intended to

509

be industrially composted.

510

https://doi.org/10.26434/chemrxiv-2025-vtczp ORCID: https://orcid.org/0000-0002-6104-8310 Content not peer-reviewed by ChemRxiv. License: CC BY 4.0

511

Today, most managed plastic waste is landfilled,72 some is incinerated, and even less is recycled

512

and composted.73 While PP is recyclable, PP straws are often not accepted by municipal recycling

513

facilities because of their size and shape, and contamination from food. In the U.S., ~63% of

514

municipal solid waste is landfilled.74 In many instances, it is the only waste management option

515

available.

516
517

The filled PP straws were claimed to biodegrade in landfills. The CaCO3 filled Resin straw was

518

advertised for disposal in landfills and to be formulated with an enzymatic package to support its

519

biodegradation in that environment.43 These additives are designed to stimulate enzymatic

520

degradation of the polymer by fouling microbes under anaerobic conditions, though their

521

effectiveness has been dubious.75,76 Collectively, the respiration, mass loss, and microbial

522

community composition analyses suggest these additives were not stimulating the biodegradation

523

of the PP matrix in aerobic marine conditions. The Agave Bagasse filled Resin straw was

524

advertised for â€œavoiding landfillsâ€ but did not mention where it was intended to go. Being PP, this

525

straw likely could only be properly disposed of by landfilling or incineration. There is little evidence

526

to support the possibility of either strawâ€™s biodegradation in landfills (i.e., no third-party certificates

527

for the products). However, taken at face value, assuming the claims hold that the filled PP straws

528

can biodegrade in terrestrial and landfill environments, their disposal in landfills would result in

529

biogenic CH4 emissions (Figure 3E). Landfills contribute ~10% of global anthropogenic CH4

530

emissions, and their leachates can diminish fresh and groundwater resources. 77 Because of

531

biogenic CH4 emissions, promoting landfilling as the managed EoL strategy can have significant

532

associated GHG emissions for all but the non-biodegradable straws (Figure 3E).

533
534

Composting can be an alternative EoL strategy. Unlike landfilling, composting is an aerobic

535

microbial degradation process that converts organic matter to biomass and CO2, not CH4. As a

536

result, the GWP of GHG emissions can be an order of magnitude less for compost than landfill

https://doi.org/10.26434/chemrxiv-2025-vtczp ORCID: https://orcid.org/0000-0002-6104-8310 Content not peer-reviewed by ChemRxiv. License: CC BY 4.0

537

(Figure 3E). Composting can be operated on the industrial scale using elevated temperatures or

538

at the individual scale using ambient conditions (i.e., home composting). Some materials are only

539

certified industrially-compostable (e.g., PLA), while others are certified home-compostable (e.g.,

540

PBAT, PHA, and CDA). For food-soiled, single-use items, composting affords a strategy well-

541

equipped to handle such waste alongside food scraps.

542
543

Nonetheless, composting has its challenges. Presently, composting is rarely practiced and lacks

544

available infrastructure at the municipal level. In the U.S., it has been estimated that only ~4% of

545

food waste is composted.78 Thus, while products are meant for and designed to be composted,

546

they may rarely follow that intended path and instead enter landfills or incinerators. Moreover,

547

composting is not without its environmental impacts, as well. Improperly aerated compost can

548

result in CH4 emissions in the same way as landfills. For composting to be broadly adopted, it will

549

require greater investment by consumers and communities in composting capabilities for

550

biodegradable plastics.

551
552

Mismanaged EoL. Drinking straws can be mismanaged at their EoL by being littered or disposed

553

of in the wrong waste stream (e.g., landfilled instead of composted). In the event of littering,

554

several of the straws will persist in the coastal ocean despite claims of their degradability in other

555

environments (e.g., compost or soil). The PP-based and Resin 1 straws were estimated to persist

556

for decades or much longer if leaked (Figure 3F). Notably, the Resin 1 straw exhibited ~10%

557

mass loss after 30 weeks of incubation (Figure 1). However, the microbial community structure

558

was similar to that of the PP-based straws (Figure 2), which is unexpected if the community was

559

adapting to metabolize PBAT. Considering this finding, further analyses of the Resin 1 material

560

are necessary to confirm whether the shallow mass loss rate after the first time point was due to

561

a residual additive slowly leaching, the slow metabolism of the polymer by fouling microbes, or

562

other processes. Conversely, because the PHA- and CDA-based straws were shown to

564

For the straws that were not persistent, their differences in estimated environmental lifetimes

565

reflected tradeoffs in material and geometric properties.

Disposal of drinking straws in landfills intended for compost results in additional and more potent

568

GHG emissions that otherwise would not have been produced. This outcome is because

569

570

576
577

Social costs were used to quantify, contextualize, and compare the environmental impacts of

578

marine plastic pollution and GHG emissions. Social costs reflect the external costs to society

579

associated with a good, service, or outcome. The exchange constant (the conversion factor of

580

581

582

583

584

of straws that leak into the environment in the U.S. annually, the switch represents a potential

585

Implications

617
618

From this case study of drinking straws, four valuable lessons were learned. First, the combination

619

of approaches taken by the PHA1 straw resulted in a product that qualitatively and quantitatively

620

could be the least impactful. It fits within a circular materials framework, had the greatest material

621

efficiency, was non-persistent in the coastal ocean, and was carbon-offsetting by utilizing diverted

622

anthropogenic CH4 emissions as a feedstock. These design decisions are a promising set that

623

could be adopted for other materials and products. Second, inorganic fillers can offset GHG at

624

EoL, and biogenic wastes can be repurposed as fillers into new products, sequestering carbon

625

as well. However, merely adding fillers to the status quo polymer (e.g., PP) will likely not produce

626

significant benefits for the environment compared to using an alternative polymer. Third, many of

627

the claims made by brands about circularity had limited supporting data, which likely will lead to

628

the mismanagement of their products and unreliable outcomes. Thus, additional certifications are

629

needed to give credibility to life cycle claims and the impacts that can result from different life

630

cycle scenarios. Lastly, improper disposal of compostable products in landfills can have sizable

631

environmental impacts, thereby warranting greater investment in infrastructure to manage

632

compostable plastics and associated food waste properly. Otherwise, many of the advantages

633

afforded by these materials will be nullified. Moving forward, from our analysis, consumers,

634

brands, and policymakers should support products that divert anthropogenic GHGs for use as

635

renewable feedstocks, promote the design of products for material efficiency and minimal

636

persistence, and bolster proper disposal of compostable products by enhancing consumer

637

education and supporting investment in industrial and home composting facilities.
