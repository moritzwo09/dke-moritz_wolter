Abstract

28

The search to unravel the origins of life on Earth pivots on recognizing environments capable
of synthesizing and preserving prebiotic organic molecules (chemical precursors to biological
systems). Prebiotic chemistry plays a crucial role in understanding the origin of life on Earth,
and terrestrial hydrothermal systems may offer the suitable environment for its emergence. In
this study, we investigated the travertine deposit of the Puga hot spring in Ladakh, India- a site
that not only mimics early Earth environments but also shares similarities with the
hydrothermal conditions proposed for early and modern-day Mars. The Puga hot spring is
situated in extreme cold location settings of Ladakh, India which is characterized by nearneutral to alkaline waters, enriched in boron, and exhibiting rapid calcium carbonate
precipitation. Such features are conducive to capturing and preserving organic molecules that
serve as molecular fossils of prebiotic processes.

We employed gas chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (GC-MS/MS) for the first time
to analyse sample scraped from both the inner and outer surfaces of the travertine deposit of
Puga hot spring. Key compounds identified include traces of Î²-alanine derivatives (amino
propanoic acid), pyran-2-thione, TMS-derivatized formamide, cyclooctasulphur (Sâ‚ˆ), and
hexadecenoic methyl ester (fatty acid). These findings suggest that both biogenic and abiotic
mechanisms may contribute to the synthesis and preservation of these molecules. We
hypothesised reaction pathways for molecules identified, which supports possibility of the
dynamic interplay between high-temperature geothermal fluids and rapid cooling in a high
UV, low-pressure environment facilitating the formation of complex prebiotic molecules.
The detection of these compounds in the Puga travertine not only supports the hypothesis that
hot spring environments could have been frameworks of early life on Earth but also highlights
their potential as analogues for extraterrestrial settings. Current study highlights the pivotal
role of hydrothermal systems in chemical evolution associated with origin of life. Our findings
provide substantial support for the hypothesis of a natural "prebiotic soup" like conditions in
Puga hot-spring, Ladakh, India, revealing that cold environmental conditions may possibly
work in synergy with hot spring conditions, playing a crucial role in effectively preserving
organic moieties within calcic matrices.

Introduction

63

The origin of life on Earth remains one of the most captivating questions in science (Pross and

64

Pascal, 2013). It's possible that the right conditions existed in places such as deep-sea

65

hydrothermal vents, geothermal hot springs, or even in hidden underground areas where water

66

flows steadily (Schaible et al., 2024). Recent studies increasingly suggest that natural settings

67

like hot springs and other hydrothermal areas might have offered the ideal mix of energy,

68

reactive chemicals, and mineral structures necessary to kickstart prebiotic chemistry (ColÃ­n-

69

GarcÃ­a et al., 2019; Damer and Deamer, 2020; Deamer et al., 2019; VillafaÃ±e-Barajas and

70

ColÃ­n-GarcÃ­a, 2021). Hot springs found on land provided the right conditions for the early

71

chemical reactions that sparked life. They were originally proposed as likely places for life's

72

beginnings because these springs can accumulate organic materials and use natural cycles of

73

wet and dry periods, driven by evaporation, to solve the â€œwater problemâ€ associated with deep

74

hydrothermal vents (Damer and Deamer, 2020; Schaible et al., 2024).

75

Hydrothermal environments are dynamic places where hot water colliding with the Earth's

76

crust creates pronounced temperature and chemical differences. These variations initiate the

77

process for the conversion of simple inorganic compounds into more complex organic

78

molecules. It is reported that blending hydrothermal fluids with other natural waters could lead

79

to the formation of diverse organic compounds such as amino acids and nucleobases that are

80

central to the idea of prebiotic chemistry (Shock and Schulte, 1998). This buildup of chemical

81

complexity is seen as a key precursor to life on early Earth.

82

Travertine formations that develop around hot springs and in rapid calcium carbonate

83

precipitation zones within hydrothermal systems serve as natural archives of ancient

84

geochemical processes while effectively preserving them against degradation over geological

85

timescales (Garcia et al., 2021; Ricketts et al., 2019). Recent studies have focused on these

86

deposits as potential insights into the chemical pathways that preceded biological life (Luo et

87

al., 2022; Martin et al., 2008). In hot spring system like Puga the precipitation is driven by the

88

cooling and degassing of mineral-rich geothermal waters. This process can capture transient

89

organic species that may have been generated by prebiotic reactions, thereby preserving a

90

molecular record that is accessible through modern analytical techniques.

91

92

conditions, drawing considerable astrobiological interest. Located high in the Indian

93

Himalayas, this site presents a unique combination of characteristics that resembles the

5.1 Biomolecules from the hot spring and their traceability towards biogenic origin.

278

The presence of various organic compounds (Fig.3) identified substantiates that the hot spring

279

associated travertine deposit hosts the ingredients for early life to be triggered. These

280

molecules can form through microbial metabolic activities routes. The presence of these

281

molecules in hot spring travertine deposits also raises intriguing possibilities about their

282

biogenic origins.

283

To streamline the description of the samples, we divided the organic compounds into groups

284

based on their chemical function/structure and their biogenic potential

285

(i)

Î²-alanine derivatives: Î²-Alanine also known as 3-amino propanoic acid is a naturally

286

occurring amino acid and a key metabolite in various biochemical pathways (Yang et

287

al., 2023). Its derivatives in travertine deposits may suggest the involvement of

288

microbial or algal processes. Î²-Alanine is often associated as a part of microbial

289

metabolic byproducts (Song et al., 2023). Its presence in the travertine sample

290

supports the possibility of biogenic activity, as microbes in hot springs often produce

291

or metabolize amino acids (Zhang et al., 2023).

292

(ii)

Pyran-2-thione: Sulphur-containing heterocyclic compounds like pyran-2-thione can

293

be linked through microbial sulphur metabolism. Certain bacteria involved in sulphur

294

cycling (e.g., sulphate-reducing bacteria) might produce such compounds as

295

intermediates or byproducts. For instance, cyanobacteria, sulphur-oxidizing bacteria,

296

and other microbial communities found in hot springs contribute to biomineralization

297

and organic compound synthesis through their metabolic processes (Runge et al.,

298

2023).

316

makes the cyclooctane molecules to be precipitated in the travertine matrix. So,

presence of cyclooctane sulphur points toward cooling associated formation
