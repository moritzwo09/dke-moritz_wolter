We studied selenium (Se) sequestration in minimally disturbed lacustrine sediments using
flow-through reactors (FTR) in response to organic matter lability, selenium (Se)
speciation and temperature (4 and 23¬∞C). Initial sediment was composed of either fresh or
aged organic matter (OM), and was fed with environmentally relevant, low Se
concentrations and filtered lake water. We monitored Se concentration as well as
speciation along with pH and the concentrations of dissolved OM, NO3-, NO2-, Fe(II),
SO42- and HS- in the outflow of FTRs during 8 experimental phases along increasing Se
concentrations. All experiments sequestered a large proportion of Se. Fresh, labile OM
removed 50% more Se than aged, more recalcitrant OM. Along with a highest proportion
of reduced redox-sensitive species in the reactors with fresh OM, this result is consistent
with reducing conditions promoting Se sequestration. Inflowing selenite was sequestered
to a larger extent than inflowing selenate. Lastly, only selenate reduction responded
strongly to temperature. At 100 nM inflow, selenate was sequestered at a rate of 92 pmol
cm-3 d-1 at 23¬∞C, which lowered to 80 pmol cm-3 d-1 at 4¬∞C. Outflow Se speciation for
selenate reduction experiments comprised mostly of organic Se species at 23¬∞C and, in
contrast, solely of selenate at 4¬∞C. We hypothesize that selenate reduction proceeded via
microbial processes, in line with reactions catalyzed by enzymes being temperature
dependent. Overall, our findings suggest that the mobilisation and warming of the boreal
and permafrost carbon pools may increase the capacity of aquatic environments to
sequester Se, lowering its bioavailability.

Introduction

46

Selenium is an essential micronutrient that has a narrow window of concentration between

47

deficiency and toxicity (Hatfield et al. 2016). In surface waters, Se transfer to the food web

48

is strongly influenced by sediment‚Äìwater interactions (Martin et al. 2022), where sediments

49

can be either sources or sinks of Se depending on the complex interplay between organic

50

carbon lability, major redox, and microbially-mediated pathways (Laberge-Carignan et al.

51

2024). Freshwater sediments and water-logged soils are key features of cold-region

52

landscapes worldwide. They are ubiquitous in vast expanses of the boreal (14 % of the land

53

surface) and permafrost (11 % of the land surface) landscapes, as well as in large

54

proportions of temperate ecoregions. Such wetlands, lakes and river systems are underlain

55

by sediments that experience seasonal cooling under the influence of 4¬∞C water which sinks

56

and accumulate at the sediment-water interface throughout the cold season (Jones et al.

57

2024).

58

Seasonally alternating temperatures, organic matter (OM) and dissolved oxygen (DO)

59

concentrations thus form the basis of hydrogeochemical and ambient redox conditions that

60

impact Se speciation and mobility. Se is a redox sensitive element with four different redox

61

states naturally found in the environment, -2, 0, +4 and +6 (Winkel et al. 2012). Selenate

62

(SeO42-), the oxyanion of Se(VI), is the most mobile Se species and the main species in

63

oxic conditions (Sharma et al. 2015). Selenite (HSeO3- or SeO32-), the oxyanion of Se(IV),

64

has a stronger affinity for adsorption onto clays and metal oxides such as ferrihydrite and

65

gibbsite, onto which it form inner-sphere complexes (Deen et al. 2022; Fern√°ndez-Mart√≠nez

66

& Charlet 2009; Goberna-Ferron et al. 2021). Selenite also as a stronger binding affinity

67

than selenate for dissolved organic matter, which provides a pathway for its sequestration

68

in sediment (Dalai et al. 2023; Sharma et al. 2015). Se species can adsorb onto pyrite, which

69

leads to the reduction of selenate to selenite at the mineral‚Äôs surface (Guida et al. 2023) and

70

the reduction of selenite to Se0(s) (Deen et al. 2022; Mitchell et al. 2013). Pyrite yields

71

higher selenite reduction rates compared to siderite and sphalerite (Deen et al. 2022),

72

making it an important reactive surface in abiotic Se reduction (Guida et al. 2023).

73

Ultimately, both selenate and selenite can be reduced to poorly soluble elemental Se0(s).

74

Collectively, the above-mentioned abiotic processes, which are considered to be at

equilibrium, can nevertheless be hindered by dissolved OM (DOM) acting as a ligand, as

76

previously shown for arsenic (As) adsorption onto Fe oxides (Couture et al. 2010).

77

OM is thus a key factor controlling Se speciation and mobility (Dalai et al. 2023;

78

Gustafsson & Johnsson 1992), both as a ligand and as an electron donor during the

79

sequential utilization of terminal electron acceptors. OM presenting a greater proportion of

80

labile moieties such as polysaccharides, proteins, organic acids, fatty acids (Poirier et al.

81

2005) will establish microbial activity to along the redox gradient (LaRowe & Van

82

Cappellen 2011), promoting electron transfer (Borch et al. 2010; Paredez et al. 2017).

83

Given that microbial processes proceed via enzymatic reactions, their rate is constrained

84

by temperature (Davidson et al. 2006; Nielsen et al. 2019). OM oxidation progressively

85

leaves more recalcitrant OM, that can act as a ligand and complex dissolved Se (Gustafsson

86

& Johnsson 1994; Li et al. 2017). In waterlogged soil and sediment, redox stratification

87

responds to the availability of OM that is labile towards microbial oxidation (Vincent et al.

88

2024). Several mechanisms, often simultaneous, account for the interactions between Se

89

and OM. These include microbial uptake and transformation (Luo et al. 2022), direct or

90

indirect complexation (Martin et al. 2022; Sharma et al. 2015), and Se reduction under

91

anoxic conditions, which are controlled by OM and temperature (Li et al. 2017; Nielsen et

92

al. 2019; Tolu et al. 2014). Ultimately, biotic selenate and selenite reduction proceeds via

93

either dissimilatory reduction to poorly soluble Se0(s) (Martin et al. 2011; Schilling et al.

94

2018), or assimilatory reduction to organo-Se, which is essential for life (Hatfield et al.

95

2016; Sharma et al. 2015).

96

The fate of Se in cold region is a concern in aquatic environments (Laberge-Carignan et al.

97

2024), agricultural settings (Pi et al. 2023), and under the influence of anthropogenic

98

activities such as coal combustion (Cooke et al. 2024). Nevertheless, and despite cold-

99

region being inherently characterised by 4¬∞C water during the cold season, data on

100

sediment Se dynamics are generally reported at growing season (e.g., room) temperature.

101

It is likely that warmer temperature will affect the sediment‚Äôs capacity to retain Se by

102

enhancing microbially favorable pathways (Wells & Stolz 2020), the dissolved organic

103

matter (DOM) composition and concentration (Porcal et al. 2015), the kinetics of chemical

reaction (Davidson et al. 2006), and the solubility of dissolved oxygen (DO) (Schladow et

105

al. 2002).

106

In addition, a wide array of Se reduction rates are obtained from microbial pure culture

107

experiments (Kausch et al. 2012; Luo et al. 2022; Yan et al. 2020), or sterile batch

108

experiments (Dalai et al. 2023; Goberna-Ferron et al. 2021; Guida et al. 2023; L√≥pez-Toyos

109

et al. 2023), under conditions far from those prevailing in the field. Such experiments result

110

in an artificially high reaction rates given the high supply of substrates for enzymatic

111

reactions (Schilling et al. 2019; Schilling et al. 2018). Thus, there exists knowledge gap on

112

the rate of Se sequestration at cold temperature in environmentally relevant conditions.

113

Here, we aim to fill this gap and provide a first estimate of the temperature control on Se

114

sequestration under such environmentally relevant conditions, specifically: i) low, nM Se

115

concentrations, ii) minimally disturbed sediment which preserves the microbial niches

116

(Pallud et al. 2007; Pi et al. 2023) and iii) under both cold (4¬∞C) and warm (23¬∞C)

117

conditions. This will allow a realistic estimate of the in situ apparent Se reaction rate,

118

relevant for the field (Schilling et al. 2018). Further to cold and warm comparison, we also

119

compared the role of OM lability as well as the behavior of both selenate and selenite as

120

Se species fed to the FTRs. The former was achieved by selected initial sediment with

121

abundant labile (i.e., fresh) organic carbon supplied with dissolved oxygen, and sediment

122

with recalcitrant (i.e., aged) carbon under anoxic conditions, while the latter was achieved

123

by adding either selenate or selenite to the input solutions and performing Se speciation

124

measurements in the outflow. To our knowledge, this is the first study to report of naturally

125

occurring Se reduction rate at both 4 and 23¬∞C, two environmentally relevant end member

126

of cold-region temperature.

Sediment and Porewater Sampling.

146

A first core was retrieved in September 2020 with a 9 cm diameter gravity corer equipped

147

with a core catcher (Uwitec, Austria) using a pre-drilled tube. This core was only used to

148

withdrawn sediment porewater every cm from the sediment-water interface down to 10 cm

149

depth using Rhizon samplers (Rhizosphere, Wageningen, Netherlands). The porewater was

150

151

Canada) yielding a final sample concentration of 4% HNO3 for major and trace element

152

analysis. In June 2022, three more cores were retrieved at the same site. One core was

153

sliced every cm, freeze-dried and used as a sediment reference for initial conditions. The

154

two other cores were used to assemble the FTRs.

156

161

Influence of Temperature on Se Reduction Rates

385

Based on the DOC chromophoric properties (Supplementary Information Figure S9), the

386

warm FTRs leached more terrestrial, old, and humic DOC compared to the inflow

387

solutions. The cold FTRs did not exhibit this behavior. This is probably the outcome of

388

enhanced enzymatic activity and OM decomposition under warmer conditions (Dick

389

2011). Similarly, the warm FTRs induced lower ratios of oxidized to reduced species for

390

the major redox couples (Fig. 4) regardless of the lability of OM, indicating greater

391

microbial activity.

392

Microbial reduction of selenate, which leads to Se removal via Se0(s) particulate formation

393

or incorporation into organic compounds (Fern√°ndez-Mart√≠nez & Charlet 2009), and Se
16

394

methylation are all known to be an enzymatic processes (Dick 2011). Increased

395

temperature increases kinetic energy, facilitating enzymatic reactions, particularly in

396

bacteria (Becker 1986; Davidson et al. 2006; Dick 2011). Microbial enzyme-catalyzed

397

reactions are thus temperature-dependent, and their rate can double with each 10¬∞C rise in

398

temperature (Davidson et al. 2006). Indeed, at ambient temperature, published FTR

399

experiments on Se reduction (Schilling et al. 2018) show that selenate-reducing capacity

400

of the microbial communities are never exceeded. Here, the higher Se reduction rates (Fig.

401

3) for the warm FTRs compared to the cold FTRs are suggestive of microbial enzyme-

402

catalyzed reactions. Microbial control is also explained by our observed variation in the

403

proportion of organic Se species in the outflow solution between the warm and cold FTRs

404

fed with selenate.

405

S√∂derlund et al. (2016) conducted batch experiments with selenite and selenate under

406

aerobic conditions to assess the impact of temperature on Se removal. They observed low

407

selenate removal, no speciation variation, and no temperature effects from 4 to 38 ¬∞C.

408

Under aerobic conditions, selenate reduction is not favorable (Borch et al. 2010;

409

Fern√°ndez-Mart√≠nez & Charlet 2009). Poor adsorption of selenate on mineral surfaces and

410

low complexation with OM explain the observations reported by S√∂derlund et al. (2016).

411

However, here the use of FTRs with undisturbed sediment that preserved microbial niches,

412

and the anoxic conditions likely facilitated selenate reduction and removal (Schilling et al.

413

2018).

Contrasting Response of Selenite and Selenate to Experimental Conditions

415

All the FTRs fed with selenite had similar normalized rates (NRSe_net; Fig. 3a). The lack of

416

a temperature influence on the FTRs fed with selenite suggest abiotic removal reactions.

417

As shown in the literature, abiotic Se reduction is not dependent on temperature (S√∂derlund

418

et al. 2016). Bruggeman et al. (2007) observed selenite reduction to organically bound Se

419

species by humic substances in abiotic laboratory batch experiments. Similarly, Dalai et al.

420

(2023) showed selenite removal by abiotic interactions with OM, which were explained by

421

the presence of Fe. In contrast, microbial selenate reduction is known as a primary Se

422

removal pathway (Wells & Stolz 2020), with its reduction by abiotic pathways being slow

(Dalai et al. 2023) or absent (Bruggeman et al. 2007; VillaRomero et al. 2013) depending

424

on the nature of the sediment.

425

We hypothesise that selenite is removed predominantly via abiotic pathway, while selenate

426

is removed predominantly via biotic pathways. Our hypothesis is based on the observation

427

that OM- and Fe-rich sediments remove selenite (

either temperature or OM lability, as shown by the similar NRSe_net_4 values between FTRs.

429

In contrast, selenate seems to be controlled by biotic pathways, consistent with the decrease

430

in NRSe_net_6 alongside a decrease in temperature (Fig. 3a). This could be ascribed to an

431

additional step needed in the reduction of selenate: most pathways for selenate reduction

432

to Se0 involve a two-step process where selenate reduction to selenite is followed by

433

selenite reduction to organo-Se or Se0(s) (Wells & Stolz 2020).

434

Previous studies reported maximal selenate reduction rates based on a regression of

435

potential rate as a function of the selenate input concentrations, following a Michaelis-

436

Menten equation (Schilling et al. 2018; Steinberg & Oremland 1990; VillaRomero et al.

437

2013). Those microcosms were not Se-limited, as the authors insured that selenate was the

438

sole terminal electron acceptor provided to the microbial community (Pallud & Van

439

Cappellen 2006; Steinberg & Oremland 1990; VillaRomero et al. 2013). Here, other

440

electron acceptors remain more abundant, such as the Se is a micronutrient and uptaken for

441

assimilatory purposes. Rates derived are thus representative of in-situ environmental rates

442

pertaining to cold regions, but not of the maximum potential for Se reduction of the

443

sediment. Those reaction rates are lower for selenate (RSe_net_6) than for selenite (RSe_net_4)

444

(Fig. 3b).

This experiment aimed to assess the impact of temperature and OM content on the mobility

508

of selenite and selenate in sediments using FTRs. Surface sediments with labile, fresh OM

509

removed 50% more Se than sediment with more recalcitrant OM. FTRs with fresh OM

510

produced lower dissolved Se efflux along more reduced electron acceptor, and more

511

degraded DOC in their outflows, suggesting higher microbial metabolism.

512

While temperature had little effect on selenite mobility at low concentrations, it

513

significantly impacted selenate sequestration rates which decreased by more than 50 %

514

under cold temperature. To our knowledge, this is the first experiment to evaluate Se

515

mobility starting with selenate or selenite at temperatures lower than 15¬∞C. We hypothesize

516

that the difference points to two distinct reduction pathways: an abiotic pathway that

517

reduces selenite, which is less affected by temperature, and a microbial pathway that

518

reduces and sequesters selenate which is affected by temperature. Cold region soils are thus

519

likely to increasingly retain Se. Those soils include peatlands in permafrost landscape,

520

organic-rich lake sediments and organic-rich cryosols susceptible to warming under

521

climate change. As a result, Se fluxes to the aquatic environment may decrease, while the

522

risk of Se deficiencies throughout the food web may increase.
